🖧 Step 1 — Before You Begin
This tool recovers your ISP connection credentials by briefly connecting your ISP router to a spare LAN port and capturing the authentication traffic.

Port assignment — important:

🔴 LAN 1 — reserved for the ISP router capture cable. Do not plug anything else into LAN 1. 💻 LAN 2 or LAN 3 — use one of these for your laptop so you can monitor progress in this wizard while the capture runs. 📶 WiFi — also fine for monitoring if you prefer.
⚠ LAN 1 will be temporarily taken offline during capture so the ISP router detects a fresh connection and re-authenticates. LAN 2, LAN 3 and WiFi are not affected. Your existing WAN connection is also unaffected until the final Apply step.
You will also need:
  • Your ISP router (powered on, or ready to be powered on)
  • An Ethernet cable long enough to reach from LAN 1 to the ISP router's WAN port
  • This laptop/device connected to LAN 2, LAN 3, or WiFi

🔗 Step 2 — Prepare LAN 1 for Capture
Before you plug the ISP router in, LAN 1 needs to be taken offline.

Why? When the ISP router's cable is first plugged in, it detects a live link and begins authenticating with the ISP — sending its credentials across the wire. That authentication handshake is exactly what we need to capture.

If LAN 1 is already live when you plug in, the ISP router may have already authenticated before the capture starts and we'll miss it. Taking the port down first means the ISP router sees a fresh connection the moment we bring it back up — guaranteed cold start.

Nothing else on your network is affected — LAN 2, LAN 3 and WiFi stay fully online.

🔌 Step 3 — Connect the ISP Router
📞/🔆 ——— 📦 ——— 🔌 ——— 🖥️
DSL/FIBRE/COAX LINE    ISP ROUTER (WAN IN, LAN OUT)    CABLE    YOUR ROUTER (LAN 1)
Do this now, in order:

1 Make sure the ISP router's external line is plugged in — this is the phone cable (DSL/ADSL), fibre ONT cable, or coax cable that comes from the wall. Without this the ISP router has nothing to authenticate to and the capture will be empty. 2 Take an Ethernet cable and plug one end into LAN 1 on your OpenWrt router. LAN 1 is currently offline so the link light will not come on yet — that's normal. 3 Plug the other end into the WAN or Internet port on your ISP router — this is usually a different colour from the LAN ports, often blue or yellow. 4 If the ISP router has been running for a while, reboot it now (unplug the power, wait 5 seconds, plug back in). A fresh boot forces it to re-authenticate from scratch, which gives us the best chance of capturing the credentials cleanly. 5 Wait for the ISP router's lights to settle — the DSL or WAN light should be solid, not flashing (about 30–60 seconds after power-on). Then click the button below.
⚠ Do not click until the ISP router's line is connected and its WAN light is solid. The authentication happens in the first few seconds of the connection — click too early and you'll miss it.

🔬 Step 5 — Auto-Testing Connection
LAN 1 has been released and your laptop's connection is restored.

The tool is now automatically testing your WAN port with the settings it captured, trying each combination in order — most likely first.

Why multiple attempts? Some ISPs require MAC address cloning (your old router's MAC is registered with them). Some use a VLAN tag. Some need both. Rather than asking you to figure that out, the tool just tries each combination until one connects.

⏱ How long will this take? Each attempt waits 30 seconds for the interface to come up, then pings 8.8.8.8. In the worst case (all combinations tried) this could take up to several minutes — you can see exactly how many are queued in the list below. You don't need to do anything — just wait.
